Practice

Routines that help every student thrive.

Learning an instrument is exciting and fun. Practicing regularly—slowly, intentionally, and with curiosity—leads to steady progress and pride in every accomplishment.

Practice requirements

Consistency matters more than marathon sessions. Aim for focused repetitions, rest, and review.

  • • Preparatory–Level 1: 30 minutes, 5 days per week (preschoolers: 20 minutes)
  • • Level 2–3: 40 minutes, 5 days per week
  • • Level 4–5: 50 minutes, 5 days per week
  • • Level 6–8: 60 minutes, 5 days per week
  • • Level 9–Advanced: 90 minutes, 5 days per week

How to work a new piece

Before playing, examine the clefs, key signature, time signature, tempo, and form. Tap rhythms and write in counting if needed. Add suggested fingerings—or create your own—and divide the piece into manageable sections.

Practice difficult measures hands separately. Analyze the chords, repeat small sections five times, and work backward from the end to strengthen memory. Include dynamics from the start; the weight you assign to the keys changes the sound.

Use a metronome to establish pulse, gradually increasing tempo. When you feel stuck, slow down. Some passages benefit from intentional memorization so you can focus fully on shaping the line.

Encouraging your child

Help students understand that music practice is a priority—similar to brushing teeth or finishing homework. Set a daily time that works best for your family (morning, after school, or before dinner) and stick with it. If a student has heavy homework, break practice into 15-minute segments between study sessions.

Young musicians may need a parent nearby a few times per week. Ask guiding questions (“What dynamics are hiding in this phrase?”) rather than giving orders. Rewards can provide short-term motivation, while self-discipline and accountability build long-term success.

Why routine matters

The brain continues solving musical puzzles during sleep. When practice is consistent, students often find yesterday’s challenge easier the next day. Irregular practice interrupts this powerful learning loop.

Above all, music should remain joyful. Celebrate every breakthrough, listen together, and attend recitals so students feel supported.
